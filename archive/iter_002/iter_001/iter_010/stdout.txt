### Key Findings

1. **Representation collapse confirmed for the current default**: `Uniform[-0.01, 0.01]` produces codes near zero (avg \|code\| ≈ 0.001), with test accuracy ≈ 19.2% — essentially **random guessing** (1/5 classes). The training loss converges to 0 because both the embedding and codes collapse to zero simultaneously.

2. **Larger initialization scale = better performance by a huge margin**: Increasing the scale from ±0.01 to N(0,1) improves test accuracy from **19.2% → 50.4%** (a +31.2 percentage point gain).

3. **The untrained random embedding is already highly informative**: For `Normal(0, 1)`, the untrained baseline achieves 51.6% — **even higher than the trained version** (50.4%). The same is true for `Normal(0, 0.5)` where untrained (41.8%) ≈ trained (41.2%). This means the random projection from the initialization alone provides useful feature separation — a form of **random kernel / random projection** benefit.

4. **Training with L1 regularization may be counter-productive**: For the top two methods (N(0,1) and N(0,0.5)), training actually *slightly reduces* accuracy vs. the untrained baseline. The L1 penalty (λ=0.002) pushes codes toward zero, degrading the useful random features.

### Conclusion for Preventing Collapse

**The primary fix for representation collapse is to increase the embedding initialization scale.** `Normal(0, 1)` is the best choice, giving 50.4% test accuracy. This works because:

- Larger initial embeddings → larger initial codes (outside the dead zone of tanh) → meaningful gradients during training
- The random initialization acts as a feature expansion (random projection) that provides linearly-separable representations even before training

Additionally, to avoid the slight degradation from training, you may want to **reduce or remove the L1 penalty** and/or **use a learning rate schedule** that preserves useful initial representations.
